  • So Much to Love About Wearable Blankets/Sleep Sacks

During the baby's first year of life, the number one source of death is SIDS, Sudden Infant Death Syndrome (Nova Scotia Health, 2016). The exact cause is mysterious. The Mayo Clinic offers some plausible explanations.

Every parent and caregiver should know how to prevent that precious bundle of joy from being suffocated while sleeping. Besides placing the baby on his/her back and keeping the crib clear of comforter, pillow, bumper, and soft toys, it is important to have a firm mattress and sleep clothing or cover that would not in any way hinder the baby's breathing.

Bodysuits seem to be the easiest and safest solution, if the bedroom temperature is appropriate. When the baby does need a cover, the Loving Care series, parenting guides compiled by the Nova Scotia Health Authority (2016), suggested knitted or crocheted blankets. Drawing from generations of folk wisdom and browsing hundreds of products on the market, we believe that swaddles and sleep sacks, or wearable blankets, are good choices to keep the little angels cozy, warm, and most importantly safe. We think that it may be a good idea to stick to bodysuits and/or wearable blankets for a couple of years.

  • Cozy and Cute, Newborn and Infant in a Swaddle

Newborns may like to be swaddled. Before the baby can turn or wave his/her arms and legs strongly, a baby may be swaddled up to a few months, particularly when using a commercial swaddle that is designed to have some leeway in its tie and provide ample room for the little legs. Often it works to choose clothing a little bigger than fits the baby for him/her to grow into it. However, we found in our experience that it does not work this way in case of swaddles, which ideally should fit perfect and relatively snug.

With a cotton blanket of the right size and thickness, you can certainly make a swaddle yourself. Here is a video demonstration by a pediatrician at Christi Health (Creative Commons License); watch up to 1:53 for how to swaddle.
