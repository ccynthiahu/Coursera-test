How to Dress Your Baby to Sleep Safe and Comfy: Sleepers, Swaddles, and Sleep Sacks

This article is for you if you have a baby under two, particularly if your baby is less than one year old, or if you are preparing to become a parent or caregiver. Surely you want your baby to sleep safely and comfortably and sleep long and well (Read about SIDS and sleep safety for babies). Based on our experience and research, we think that you can select the following baby clothes or combine them in layers to dress your baby for sleep, as is appropriate for the nursery temperature and your baby’s specific needs.

2. Sleeper: Standard Baby Pajamas

Bodysuit and sleeper work hand in hand on different occasions in a baby’s early life, including sleeping in them. Their advantage is one piece, with a long zipper or line of snaps, simple to put on the baby and easy for making diaper change.

Footed sleepers were our favorite when our little ones often slept and played in them. Little babies do not care about what they wear yet, and if they were like ours, they would not appreciate "fuss" over getting dressed.

Some babies appreciate footless sleepers, if their feet tend to be hot, particularly when they have a sleep sack as the outer layer. Footless sleepers can also pair with socks and/or shoes during the day.

3. Swaddle: Great for Newborns

Some newborns like to be swaddled. Before he/she can turn or wave his/her arms and legs strongly, a baby may be swaddled up to a few months (about 3 or 4 months), particularly when using a commercial swaddle that is designed to have some leeway in its tie and provide ample room for little legs. Often it works to choose clothing a little bigger than fits the baby for him/her to grow into it. However, we found in our experience that it does not work this way in case of swaddles, which ideally should fit perfect and relatively snug.

With a cotton blanket of the right size and thickness, you can certainly make a swaddle yourself. Here is a video demonstration by a pediatrician at Christi Health (Creative Commons License); watch up to 1:53 for how to swaddle.

What Should Your Baby Wear Underneath the Sleep Sack?

Most sleep sacks are sleeveless. This makes it necessary and a good idea to layer the baby properly.

When the nursery temperature is 77℉ (25℃) and above, the baby may not need layers and may only need a thin layer of cotton or cotton muslin, be it swaddle, sleeper, gown, or sleep sack. If it is sweltering, even a bodysuit may suffice.

If the nursery is under 77℉ (25℃), but is still at least 68℉ (20℃), consider layering. Ideally the baby sleeps in a merino wool sleep sack, and he/she could wear bodysuit (short-sleeve or long-sleeve), sleeper (footless or footed), or gown underneath. When the room is on the cool side, layer both bodysuit and sleeper under the sack.

In case the nursery temperature is less than 68℉ (20℃), the merino sleep sack could work marvels. The baby can have both long-sleeve bodysuit and footed sleeper underneath.

Rule-of-Thumb Suggestions on How to Dress Your Baby for Sleep

The overall rules of thumb are as follows:

First, dress in layers. Even cotton is warmer and breathable when layered up.

Second, it is safer to err on the side of underdress than overdress, because overdressing is shown by research to be related to SIDS (healthline).

Third, each baby is unique, and there is no good-for-all solution. Babies grow at different rates and have their own unique needs. You are the one who knows your baby the best. Work out the best solution with your baby.

Fourth, monitor the nursery temperature and your baby’s body temperature to help establish good practices. The baby’s sleep environment should not be too hot. Woolino recommends that the nursery temperature should range between 64-68℉ (18-20℃). We keep our bedrooms between 68-70℉ (20-21℃) in winter. In summer, the bedroom could get quite hot. We use fans to cool it down and move from the second to the first floor in the shady corner and normally keep the bedroom at 77-79℉ (25-26℃) on the hottest days. Also check your baby's body temperature, which should normally be around 97.5℉ (36.4℃). In case it reads 100℉ (38℃) or above, he or she is running a high temperature or fever (NHS). However, you may get slightly different readings via different types of thermometers (Mayo Clinic).

Does swaddling work for all newborns?

No, some babies fuss and wiggle out of a swaddle. Consider the bodysuit and sleeper combination or add a sleep sack when needed. If your baby gets the merino wool sleeper or sleep sack, he or she will be comfortable through the seasons.

Do sleep sacks work for every baby?

For most babies, a sleep sack would not wiggle up to the neck and face, unless the sack is too big and loose. Of the hundreds of comments we came through, there were a few that mentioned the scenario. When that happens, it beats the purpose of keeping your precious safe. Consider other ways to dress your baby, such as swaddling for newborns, or a bodysuit and sleeper combination or a sleeper gown, and retry the sleep sack when it fits better.

How do you know if you dress your baby right?

Observe: Does your baby look well-rested, happy, and alert? If so, you are doing things right. Do you see blush or feel sweat on your baby? If so, your baby is hot. Remove a layer or some clothing item. Do you see bluish veins on the skin? If so, your baby is a bit cold. Add a layer, switch to warmer clothes, and warm him/her up. If you suspect anything more serious, call your doctor or 911.
